What has to happen, in orderSix phases, sign-off, and where each one goes wrong▾

Each phase consumes the output of the one before it, so running them out of order produces an assessment you cannot defend. Record who performed, reviewed and approved each one — the reviewer and approver must not be the person who did the work, because a self-assessed readiness review is the first thing an audit firm discounts.

Where is the gap assessment? It is not one phase. Phase 2 surfaces mapping gaps, phase 3 surfaces design gaps, phase 4 surfaces evidence gaps, and phase 5 is where all three are classified, rated and turned into a backlog. If you are asked when the gap assessment happens, the answer is phases 2 to 5 — and phase 5 is when it becomes a document you can hand someone.

Stage 3 is the trap. Controls sitting at stage 3 feel finished to everyone involved — the work is genuinely happening — and they are the ones that produce findings, because the auditor cannot test what cannot be enumerated. When you rate a control, rate the evidence, not the activity.
