How SS and ROP are readjusted

The statistical baseline. Safety stock exists to buffer demand variability over replenishment lead time at a chosen service level. Reorder point is average lead-time demand plus that buffer:

SS = Z × σd × √LT
ROP = (d̄ × LT) + SS

Z = service-level factor (98% → 2.05, 95% → 1.65, 90% → 1.28) · σd = std-dev of period demand · d̄ = mean period demand · LT = lead time in periods.

The rule that matters here: when trailing 12-month consumption is zero, both terms collapse — d̄ = 0 and σd = 0, so SS = 0 and ROP = 0 by the math itself. Any safety stock still parameterized on these materials is a legacy setting, not a calculation. That's why the table drives most items to zero and converts genuinely-needed-but-rare items to make-to-order (MTO) or vendor consignment.

Worked example — SP-4415 hydraulic seal kit:

Old parameters (set 2022): SS = 40, ROP = 65, on-hand 58 units at $86 → $4,988 locked, ~$1,097/yr carrying cost.

Trailing 12-month demand: 0 issues. Machine it served was decommissioned in the 2024 line upgrade.

Recalc: d̄ = 0 → SS = 0, ROP = 0. Action: retain 2 units as insurance spares under a separate spares policy (criticality-based, not forecast-based), liquidate 56 via surplus broker → recover ~30¢/$, release ~$1,440 cash and ~$1,000/yr holding cost.

Insurance spares are the honest exception: zero-demand ≠ zero-need for single-point-of-failure parts. Gate those through an engineering criticality review — never through the forecast formula.

Reducing stocking cost at scale — Celonis and Aera

Celonis — find why excess stock keeps being created

Process mining · diagnosis layer
  • Mines actual ERP event logs (P2P, inventory movements) and reconstructs how stock really flows — not how the SOP says it flows.
  • Root-cause detection: surfaces the behaviors that create dead stock — duplicate materials under different numbers, maverick buying outside contracts, orders placed despite sufficient stock, SS/ROP parameters untouched for years while demand shifted.
  • Working-capital apps flag materials where days-on-hand and parameter drift diverge, and quantify the cash release available per material/plant.
  • Continuous monitoring: once cleaned, alerts fire when a buyer re-creates the pattern (e.g., PO raised on a zero-consumption item).

Vendor case studies typically report 10–20% excess-inventory reduction from parameter cleanup and process conformance. Treat published figures as directional.

Aera — decide and execute the parameter changes

Decision intelligence · action layer
  • Aera Decision Cloud continuously recalculates SS/ROP per SKU-location from live demand, variability, and lead-time data — instead of annual batch reviews.
  • Recommendations with dollar impact: each proposed change ("cut SS 40 → 0 on SP-4415, release $4.9K") is scored, explained, and routed for approval.
  • Closed loop: approved decisions write back to the ERP (SAP MRP views) automatically — the step most excess-stock programs never finish, which is why the same dead stock reappears every audit.
  • Learning: tracks which recommendations planners accept/override and tunes future proposals accordingly.

Pattern to copy even without either tool: diagnose root causes (Celonis role), then automate parameter recalculation with write-back and human approval gates (Aera role). The gap between "report" and "write-back" is where most programs die.

90-day action playbook

  1. Extract & verify (weeks 1–2): pull 24 months of goods issues per material/plant; flag zero-consumption ≥ 12 months. Verify against open reservations and BOM usage — some "dead" stock is allocated to a project.
  2. Segment (weeks 2–4): insurance spares (engineering review) · project residuals (return/restock to vendor) · duplicates (merge material masters) · true dead stock (liquidate/scrap with finance write-down approval).
  3. Reset parameters (weeks 4–6): SS = 0 / ROP = 0 on zero-demand items; convert sporadic-demand items to MTO or lower service level (98% → 90% cuts SS by ~37% at the same σ and LT); document every change for audit.
  4. Recover cash (weeks 6–12): vendor buy-backs, surplus brokers, internal transfers between plants before external sale; scrap last, with write-off approvals.
  5. Prevent recurrence (ongoing): monthly zero-consumption report to planners, block PO creation on flagged materials without override, and parameter recalculation on a quarterly cadence — manually, or via the Celonis→Aera pattern above.
